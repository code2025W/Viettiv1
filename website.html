Dr. Eleanor Ardel Vietti

Physician, Missionary, and America's First Female POW in Vietnam

Early Life

Eleanor Ardel Vietti was born in 1927 in Fort Worth, Texas. She had a twin sister named Teresa J. and a younger brother, Victor. From an early age, Eleanor was known for her compassion, intellect, and strong sense of duty. These qualities guided her toward a career in medicine and humanitarian work.

Medical Career and Missionary Work

After becoming a licensed physician, Dr. Vietti joined the Christian and Missionary Alliance (C&MA), a Protestant organization dedicated to spreading both spiritual and medical help around the world. She was assigned to a mission hospital in Ban Me Thuot, located in South Vietnam’s Central Highlands.

There, she focused much of her work on treating patients suffering from **leprosy** (Hansen’s disease), a condition that was heavily stigmatized and underserved. Dr. Vietti treated her patients with kindness, skill, and dignity — often under difficult conditions with limited resources.

Decision to Stay — and Her Disappearance

As tensions escalated during the Vietnam War, many foreigners were urged to evacuate the region. Despite the growing danger, Dr. Vietti chose to stay, saying her patients needed her. This decision reflected her selfless character and unwavering dedication to her mission.

On May 30, 1962, while preparing for surgery at the hospital, she was kidnapped by Viet Cong guerrillas along with two other missionaries — Archie E. Mitchell and Daniel A. Gerber. She was **never seen again**. No confirmed information about her fate has ever been discovered.

Legacy and Recognition

  • Dr. Vietti is considered **America's first female prisoner of war in Vietnam**.
  • Her story is remembered as one of sacrifice, courage, and deep humanitarian spirit.
  • Though her ultimate fate remains unknown, her work touched countless lives.
  • She is honored in memorials dedicated to American POWs and missing persons.

Her life continues to inspire those in the fields of medicine, faith, and international service. She represents the powerful impact one person can make through compassion and conviction.
